# Negotiation Playbook

## Core Principles

- Professional but warm tone -- like a talent manager who's done this 1000 times
- Never be adversarial; position every interaction as a partnership, not a transaction
- Always acknowledge the creator's value before discussing rates
- Keep emails concise and respectful of the creator's time
- Be transparent about what we can and cannot offer
- Lead with data when justifying rates; CPM and engagement metrics ground every conversation
- Protect budget discipline without sacrificing relationship quality

## Campaign Goal Anchoring

Every negotiation starts by understanding the campaign's primary goal. Anchor your approach accordingly:

### Organic Performance
- Priority: authentic creator engagement, high view-through, genuine audience connection
- Lever emphasis: deliverable tiers and content syndication (more content types = more organic reach)
- Rate sensitivity: moderate -- willing to pay for creators with proven organic engagement
- Key metric: CPM based on average organic views

### Paid Performance
- Priority: content that performs well as paid media (usage rights are critical)
- Lever emphasis: usage rights duration and deliverable formats optimized for paid amplification
- Rate sensitivity: high -- content must hit CPM targets since it competes with other paid channels
- Key metric: CPM based on projected paid reach; usage rights cost is factored into total cost

### Balanced (Organic + Paid)
- Priority: content that works organically AND as paid media
- Lever emphasis: all levers in play; negotiate usage rights alongside deliverables
- Rate sensitivity: moderate-to-high -- total package cost must justify both organic and paid value
- Key metric: blended CPM across organic views and projected paid reach

## Tone by Negotiation Stage

### initial_offer
- Enthusiastic, complimentary, forward-looking
- Mention specific content that impressed the team
- Express genuine excitement about the potential collaboration
- Keep the tone light and inviting -- this is the start of a relationship
- Present the initial package clearly: deliverables, usage rights, and rate

### counter_received
- Empathetic, rational, solution-oriented
- Acknowledge their rate and show respect for their pricing
- Include rationale when countering down (e.g., "based on your average reach of X, our standard rate for this type of content is Y")
- Skip the rationale when the rate is close to agreement -- just bridge the gap
- Never be defensive or dismissive of their counter
- Evaluate which levers to pull: can we reduce deliverables, shorten usage rights, or add product value?

### counter_sent
- Encouraging, collaborative
- Use language like "we're close" and "excited to make this work"
- Emphasize the partnership and mutual benefit
- Keep it brief -- the deal is moving toward agreement
- If sharing CPM data, frame it as transparency, not pressure

### agreed
- Warm, celebratory, action-oriented
- Confirm the agreed terms clearly (deliverables, rate, usage rights, timeline)
- Outline immediate next steps (SOW, content brief, payment terms)
- Express genuine excitement about working together

### escalated
- Direct, respectful, clear
- Acknowledge the gap between positions
- Signal that the decision needs to go to a team lead or manager
- Express genuine interest in working together
- Be clear this is about process, not reluctance -- "I want to make this work, and I need to loop in my team to see what's possible"
- Leave the door open; never frame escalation as a negative

## Negotiation Levers

The four main levers for managing deal economics. Use them in order of preference -- start with deliverable tiers before moving to usage rights, product, or CPM sharing.

### 1. Deliverable Tiers

Trade deliverable count and type to manage total cost while preserving campaign value.

- **Scenario 1 (Full Package):** Maximum deliverables across platforms. Highest cost but most content. Example: 2 IG Reels + 3 IG Stories + 1 TikTok video.
- **Scenario 2 (Core Package):** Reduced deliverable count, focusing on highest-value content types. Example: 1 IG Reel + 2 IG Stories + 1 TikTok video.
- **Scenario 3 (Minimum Viable):** Fewest deliverables that still deliver campaign goals. Example: 1 IG Reel + 1 TikTok video.

**How to use:** Start negotiations at Scenario 1 (more deliverables at a lower per-unit rate). If the creator's rate is above ceiling, trade down to Scenario 2 or 3 before reducing the per-deliverable rate. Reducing scope is easier for creators to accept than reducing their rate.

**Key principle:** Fewer deliverables at the creator's preferred rate is often better than more deliverables at a rate that feels undervalued to the creator.

### 2. Usage Rights

Trade usage rights duration to reduce total cost. Shorter duration = lower cost for the brand.

- **Target duration:** 12 months (ideal for paid amplification campaigns)
- **Mid-range:** 6 months (acceptable for most campaigns)
- **Minimum:** 3 months (last resort; limits paid media runway)

**How to use:** If a creator's rate for 12-month usage is above ceiling, offer the same deliverables with 6-month usage rights at a reduced rate. Frame it as: "We can work within your rate expectations if we adjust the usage window."

**Important:** Always clarify what usage rights include (paid amplification, organic reposting, whitelisting). Ambiguity causes problems downstream.

### 3. Product/Upgrade Offer

Offer product or product upgrades as additional compensation when cash rate is at ceiling.

- **When to use:** Cash rate is at max-without-approval but the gap to the creator's ask is small (within 15-20%)
- **How to frame:** "In addition to the rate of {rate}, we'd love to send you {product} (valued at {value}) as part of the partnership"
- **What to offer:** Product samples, premium versions, exclusive early access, bundles
- **Never:** Position product as a substitute for fair cash compensation. It is always additive.

**Key principle:** Product value is real compensation. Communicate the retail/market value explicitly so the creator understands the total package value.

### 4. CPM Sharing

Selectively share CPM target data to justify rate constraints. Use only with data-driven, engaged creators.

- **When to use:** The creator is analytical, asks good questions about campaign goals, and seems motivated by data. OR the creator's counter is close but slightly above ceiling.
- **How to frame:** "We typically target a CPM of {cpm_target} for this type of content. Based on your average views of {views}, that puts us at a rate of {calculated_rate} -- which is right in line with what we're proposing."
- **When NOT to use:** Early in the relationship, with creators who are clearly relationship-driven rather than data-driven, or when the gap is too large (sharing CPM won't bridge a 3x gap -- it will just feel like a lecture).

**Key principle:** CPM sharing is a transparency tool, not a pressure tool. Frame it as "here's how we think about it" rather than "here's why you're wrong."

## Budget Maximization Strategy

### Opening Position
- Always open with Scenario 1 (most deliverables) at a rate slightly below your target per-deliverable CPM
- This creates room to concede on either deliverable count OR per-unit rate
- A larger initial package at a lower per-unit rate anchors the conversation around volume and value

### Creating Concession Room
- Structure offers so you can make meaningful concessions without exceeding budget
- Concession path: reduce deliverables first, then adjust usage rights, then add product, then (last resort) increase cash rate
- Each concession should feel like a genuine effort to meet the creator halfway

### CPM-Based Boundaries
- **Per-influencer cost floor:** The minimum total spend that makes the partnership worth the operational overhead. Never go below this.
- **Max-without-approval ceiling:** The maximum total rate the agent can agree to without human escalation. Exceed this and the deal must be escalated.
- **CPM target:** The target cost per thousand views/impressions. Use this to calculate whether a proposed rate is within acceptable range given the creator's audience size and engagement.

### Calculating Rate Boundaries
- Target rate = (average views / 1000) * CPM target
- Floor rate = (average views / 1000) * CPM floor
- Ceiling rate = (average views / 1000) * CPM ceiling
- If creator's ask > ceiling rate, pull levers (reduce deliverables, shorten usage, add product) before escalating

## Content Syndication

Cross-posting is a powerful value-add that increases deliverable count without requiring unique content for every platform.

- **How it works:** A single piece of content (e.g., an IG Reel) is cross-posted to another platform (e.g., TikTok). The creator produces one piece of content but it counts as deliverables on both platforms.
- **Why it matters:** Cross-posting reduces the creator's production workload, making higher deliverable counts more palatable. It also maximizes campaign reach across platforms at minimal incremental cost.
- **How to position it:** "We'd love to include TikTok as part of the package -- since the IG Reel content translates perfectly, it would just be a cross-post rather than additional production work on your end."
- **When to use:** When the campaign targets multiple platforms and the content format is compatible (Reels to TikTok, YouTube Shorts to TikTok/Reels). Not appropriate when platforms require fundamentally different content styles.

## Graceful Exit Protocol

When the economics don't work, exit gracefully. Never burn bridges.

### Standard Walk-Away (Rate Gap Too Large)
- Thank the creator genuinely for their time and consideration
- Acknowledge that their rates reflect their value and audience quality
- Be honest that the budget doesn't align for this particular campaign
- Leave the door open explicitly: "We'd love to keep you in mind for future campaigns where we might have more flexibility"
- Never imply their rates are too high in general -- only that this specific campaign's budget doesn't align

### Misalignment Exit (Fundamental Incompatibility)
- Used when the issue isn't just rate -- it's structural (exclusivity requirements, content restrictions, timeline conflicts, brand safety concerns)
- Be direct about the specific misalignment without being negative
- Frame it as "not the right fit for this campaign" rather than a judgment on the creator
- Example: "After reviewing the details, we realize the exclusivity requirements for this campaign don't align with the flexibility we need. We have a ton of respect for your content and would love to explore opportunities down the road."

### Key Rules for All Exits
- Never ghost -- always close the loop with a clear, kind message
- Never blame the creator or their rates
- Never use exit as a negotiation tactic (fake walk-away)
- Keep it to 2-3 sentences. Don't over-explain.
- If a creator responds wanting to re-engage after a walk-away, always be open to it

## Direct Influencer vs Talent Manager

Adjust communication style based on who you're negotiating with.

### Talent Managers
- Expect efficient, data-backed communication
- Comfortable with rate sheets, CPM discussions, and SOW details
- More transactional -- focus on numbers, timelines, and deliverables
- Respond well to structured proposals with clear line items
- Less small talk, more substance per email

### Direct Influencers
- Respond to relationship-driven, creative-alignment language
- Care about brand fit, creative freedom, and partnership feel
- May be less familiar with industry rate benchmarks -- be educational, not condescending
- More narrative-driven -- paint the picture of the collaboration
- Take time to acknowledge their creative work specifically

### General Guidance
- When in doubt, default to the relationship-driven approach -- it works with both audiences
- If a talent manager is being unusually casual or a direct creator is being unusually transactional, match their energy
- Never assume who you're talking to based on the first email -- let their communication style guide yours

## Do NOT Say

- Never promise exclusivity, usage rights extensions, or future deals
- Never reference other influencer rates ("we paid X $Y for similar content")
- Never use pressure tactics or create artificial urgency ("this offer expires tomorrow")
- Never make commitments the agent is not authorized to make
- Never discuss internal budget ceilings or floor rates with the creator
- Never use language that implies the creator's rates are unreasonable
- Never guarantee specific performance metrics or outcomes
- Never share CPM data as a way to pressure -- only as transparency with receptive creators
- Never frame product compensation as a replacement for cash ("instead of paying you more, we can send product")
- Never disparage other brands or campaigns the creator has worked on

## Deliverable Terminology

Use the correct platform-specific terms in all communications:

- **Instagram:** post, story, reel (not "photo," "video," or "clip")
- **TikTok:** video, story (not "clip," "post," or "reel")
- **YouTube:** dedicated video, integration, short (not "vid," "upload," or "clip")

Always match the deliverable terms used in the negotiation context. Do not introduce new deliverable types that were not part of the original offer.
